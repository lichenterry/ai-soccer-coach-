I was at the Jefferson Cup this spring with my 13-year-old, sitting on a fold-up chair eating a granola bar, when I had the conversation that started this whole thing.

The moms next to me each had a son a few years older than mine, both in the thick of high school recruiting. They were halfway through their first year of "real" college recruiting — emails, highlight video, a couple of ID camps. And what they kept saying, over and over, was: *we have no idea if we're doing any of this right.* They'd paid for recruiting services that mostly sent generic emails. They'd watched YouTube videos that contradicted each other. The NCAA's own pages read like legal compliance documents, because that's what they are. One of them said something I haven't been able to stop thinking about since:

> "It's a black box. We're piecing together a puzzle with no picture on the box."

My older son is in MLS Next Academy. My younger one is 10 and in the Northeast Academy League, which is one of the regional on-ramps into the bigger leagues. We're not in the recruiting window yet — but I'm a product manager by day, and I went home from that tournament with a knot in my stomach about what was coming for our family in three years. I started Googling. I read every NCSA article. I asked older parents at every tournament. And here's what I realized: the information *is* out there. It's just scattered across forums and blog posts, gated behind recruiting services that won't tell you the price until you sit through a sales call, or buried in pages of NCAA compliance language. There's no one place that lays it out clearly, for free, without a sales pitch at the end.

So I built one. And the more I worked on it, the more I thought: the post I really wish someone had handed me at Jefferson Cup would also be free, also plain-English, and would just answer the question "what should we actually be doing each year." So here's that post too.

Quick honesty check before we start. Of all the U.S. high school boys who play soccer, roughly **6–7% go on to play any level of college soccer** — D1, D2, D3, or NAIA combined. At D1 specifically, the odds for boys are about **1 in 100**. That's not meant to scare you. It's meant to keep us all sane. The youth soccer industry has a way of making every family feel like it's D1 or nothing, and it isn't. Most boys who play college soccer don't play D1, and that's still a great outcome.

## Freshman year (9th grade): The year that quietly matters most

My older son is one year out from this, and the more I've researched it the more I think 9th grade is the most underrated year in the whole timeline. Nobody's calling. No coach can contact you. So it's easy to think nothing matters yet. The opposite is true — the boring stuff you do this year sets everything else up.

**Grades, grades, grades.** A 3.5+ GPA opens roughly twice as many doors as a 3.0, and at the academically selective D3 schools that don't even offer athletic money, grades are *the* recruiting work. My older son had a strong fall semester and I had a real moment of "ok, that one report card just earned him about thirty more schools."

**Pick the right team, not the highest-ranked one.** A boy who plays 60 minutes a game on a strong club team is in a better recruiting position than a benchwarmer on an MLS Next roster. I think about this a lot for my younger one. The minutes matter way more than the patch on the jersey.

**Have the actual conversation at home.** Does your kid actually want to play college soccer? Or do you want it for them? My boys are 13 and 10 and I'm trying really hard to keep checking in on this one. The answer can change anything that follows.

**What I won't do this year:** pay for a highlight video. Game speed at 14 doesn't translate to anything a college coach cares about. The video you make at 16 will be the one that matters.

## Sophomore year (10th grade): The year the rules start to apply

This is where the official NCAA rules kick in. The biggest date to know: **June 15 after sophomore year**. That's when D1 and D2 coaches can start contacting your athlete directly — calls, texts, emails, verbal offers. For boys' soccer, this is when real D1 conversations begin. If your son is on a D1 track, coaches have probably been watching him for months through his club coach by then.

What I'm planning for when my older son gets here:

**A real highlight video.** Three to five minutes, eight to twelve plays, best stuff at the front. The thing I keep reading: **college coaches decide in under 60 seconds whether to keep watching.** The first thirty seconds matter more than everything else combined. Game footage only. No training drills. Position-specific: midfielders show both feet, a defensive recovery, one play under pressure. Forwards show finishing variety. Defenders show recovery speed and aerial duels. (For a full breakdown by position, I wrote a separate guide: [what college coaches look for in a soccer highlight video](/blog/highlight-video-by-position).)

**A realistic target list.** Every parent I've talked to says they got this wrong at first. They started emailing top-25 D1 programs that were never going to recruit their kid. The right list is 12 to 15 schools across two or three divisions, calibrated to where your athlete actually is — not where you wish they were.

**The first coach email.** Short. Three sentences. Subject line: "Class of [year], position, club." Highlight video link. GPA. That's it. Coaches don't read long ones.

**Two or three showcases or ID camps.** Showcases give breadth. ID camps give depth — one school's coaches actually evaluating your kid for the better part of a day. Most families I've asked said ID camps moved their recruiting forward more.

## Junior year (11th grade): What I'm preparing for

I'm not here yet, but a big rule changed under our feet recently that almost nobody seems to know about. Starting in **2025–26, D1 schools can now offer up to 28 soccer scholarships.** The old limit for D1 men's soccer was 9.9 — meaning programs split a partial-equivalent of ten full rides across the whole roster. Soccer is still equivalency-funded, so most awards are still partial, but the new cap gives coaches a lot more flexibility. Don't assume the numbers an older neighbor's kid heard three years ago still apply.

What the older families have told me to prep for:

**Updated highlight video.** New footage from junior club season. Position-specific. Same 60-second rule.

**Official and unofficial visits.** This one I had completely wrong from old internet articles. **As of 2018, both official and unofficial visits with the coach are restricted to August 1 before junior year.** You can walk around a campus before that — that's fine. But the recruiting visit, where you meet the coach, has to wait.

**The financial conversation at home.** The average D1 men's soccer scholarship in 2024 was about $17,700 — not nothing, but nowhere close to full cost at most schools. D3 doesn't offer athletic money but often layers academic and need-based aid into a competitive package. Run actual numbers on three target schools *before* getting emotionally attached to any of them.

## Senior year (12th grade): What I've heard from families on the other side

Most D1 commits for boys happen by fall of senior year, sometimes earlier. But here's something almost nobody told me: **D2, D3, and NAIA recruiting runs all the way through spring.** I asked a mom in our club whose son just signed with a great D2 program — he committed in March of his senior year. The "we missed our window" feeling is usually wrong.

The closing-stage steps the families on the other side described:

- Narrow to four schools by early fall.
- One or two more visits at the top finalists.
- The academic decision as a tiebreaker if soccer is similar at two programs. Soccer careers end at 22. The school feeds the next forty years.
- NLI signing (D1/D2) or commitment letter (D3/NAIA).

The night before signing day, multiple families told me they still weren't fully sure. That's normal. The certainty you see on Instagram posts is mostly performance.

## Frequently asked questions

**When can college coaches officially contact my son?**
For D1 and D2 soccer, direct contact (calls, texts, emails, verbal offers) starts June 15 after sophomore year. D3 and NAIA can email anytime — there are no contact-date restrictions at those levels. Coaches at any level can send generic camp invitations earlier than June 15.

**Is it too late to start recruiting in junior year?**
For D2, D3, and NAIA — absolutely not. Junior-year starts are common at these levels and often lead to great fits. For D1 boys, it's harder but possible, especially for late-blooming athletes who jumped a club tier between sophomore and junior year.

**What if my son plays club but not MLS Next or ECNL Boys?**
You can absolutely still get recruited. Plenty of D1 athletes come from regional leagues like NAL and other pre-academy programs — the path is just more proactive (more emails, more highlight video outreach). D2, D3, and NAIA coaches recruit broadly across all club tiers and won't hold the league name against you.

**How big are men's soccer scholarships actually?**
The NCAA eliminated the old 9.9 D1 men's limit starting in 2025–26 — D1 programs can now offer up to 28. But soccer is still equivalency-funded, so most awards are still partial. The 2024 average for men's D1 soccer was around $17,700. D3 doesn't offer athletic scholarships at all, but often combines academic and need-based aid into a competitive package — sometimes more affordable than a partial D1 offer.
