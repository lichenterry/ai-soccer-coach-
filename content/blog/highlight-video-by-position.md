A few months into my research on the college soccer recruiting process (which I wrote about in [the year-by-year guide for boy moms](/blog/recruiting-timeline-by-grade)), I started asking every older parent at MLS Next tournaments the same question:

*If you could go back, what would you change about your kid's highlight video?*

The answers were almost identical. Three patterns kept showing up:

1. **The video was too long.** Five-minute videos lose coaches in the first 60 seconds.
2. **The video didn't show position-specific stuff.** Forwards showed only goals. Defenders showed almost nothing useful.
3. **The video was made too early.** Footage from age 14 doesn't represent the player at age 16.

So this is what I've put together from those conversations, plus what I've read from college coaches who post directly about what they look for. Universal rules first — the stuff that applies to every kid. Then position by position.

## Universal rules: the first 30 seconds decide everything

Every highlight video has the same job: keep a busy college coach watching for the next 4 minutes. They get hundreds of these. Most don't get a second look. The rules:

- **Length: 3 to 5 minutes.** Period. Longer and they stop watching.
- **Best clips in the first 30 seconds.** This is the most important rule in the whole post. College coaches decide within 60 seconds whether your kid is worth more attention. If your kid's best moment is at minute 4, no one will see it. Lead with your best stuff.
- **Game footage only.** No training drills. No skills moves in the backyard. No friendly scrimmages where the level is unclear. Real games, real opponents, real competitive context.
- **Highlight the player.** A small circle or arrow on your kid for each clip. Their jersey color and number obvious in every clip. Coaches don't have time to figure out which one your kid is.
- **Multiple games, multiple opponents.** Not all clips from one good game. Coaches want to see consistency across opponents and conditions.
- **Roughly 20–25 clips for field players** (fewer, longer clips for keepers). That's about 8–12 seconds per clip — long enough to show context, short enough to keep momentum.
- **Recent footage.** Within the last 6 months. Older clips suggest the player hasn't progressed.
- **Skip the music.** Most coaches mute videos by default. At best neutral, at worst distracting.
- **No long intro graphics.** Logo, name, position, class year, GPA, height/weight, contact info — five seconds maximum. Then into the action.

Now the position-specific stuff.

## Forwards

College forwards are evaluated on goal-scoring, but the goals alone aren't enough — coaches need to see *how* you scored and what you did *between* goals.

**Show:**
- **Goal variety.** 1v1 finishes, headers, finishes with your weaker foot, shots from the edge of the box. Three of the same kind of finish tells coaches you're a one-trick striker.
- **Composure in the box.** A still-on-the-ball moment under pressure says more than a thunderous shot from 20 yards.
- **Off-ball movement.** The run that creates the goal is sometimes more impressive than the finish. Show a clip where you make a defender go the wrong way.
- **First touch under pressure** — receiving with a defender on your back and turning.
- **Hold-up play** if you're a target forward — chest control, laying balls off, winning aerials.

**Skip:**
- Goals that came from a defender's mistake or an empty net.
- Endless tap-ins.
- Slow-motion of one finish stretched across 30 seconds.

## Midfielders

This is the position where college coaches are most ruthless about what's missing. They want to see versatility.

**Show:**
- **Both feet, both feet, both feet.** A midfielder who can only use one foot is a one-dimensional midfielder. Include at least 2–3 clips that obviously use your weaker foot — a switch of play, a pass under pressure, a shot.
- **Passing range.** Short combinations under pressure, plus at least one cross-field switch. Coaches want to see vision.
- **Defensive recovery.** A clip where you lose possession and immediately track back to win it. Modern coaches do not want passengers in the midfield.
- **Decision-making in tight space.** Receiving with multiple defenders close and finding the right pass.
- **For attacking midfielders:** through balls, between-the-lines movement, late runs into the box.
- **For defensive midfielders:** interceptions, screening the back four, distribution out from deep.

**Skip:**
- 8 clips of free kicks. (Most coaches skip the set-piece reel.)
- Skills moves with no purpose. A nutmeg in the middle of the field that didn't lead anywhere isn't a highlight.

## Defenders

Defenders get the worst highlight video advice. Most defender videos look like forward videos with the wrong player — clips of goals from set pieces or rare overlapping runs. Coaches see through this immediately.

**Show:**
- **1v1 defending.** Multiple clips of you closing space, jockeying, and either winning the ball or forcing the attacker into a bad pass. The most underrated thing a defender can show.
- **Recovery speed.** A clip where you start behind a runner and catch them. Defenders without recovery speed have a ceiling.
- **Aerial duels** — winning headers from goal kicks, corners, and crosses. (Center backs especially.)
- **Distribution.** Modern centerbacks pass. Show 2–3 clips of you receiving from the keeper and finding a midfielder under pressure. Long balls into space if you can hit them.
- **For fullbacks:** attacking width, overlapping runs, crosses. Crosses don't have to be perfect — coaches want to see that you can deliver.
- **Reading the game** — interceptions where you stepped in before the pass arrived.

**Skip:**
- Goals from set pieces as the whole video. They're a bonus, not the spine.
- Clips where you don't have to do much. (Coaches notice when your team was dominant and the highlight is just easy possessions.)

## Goalkeepers

Goalkeepers need to show variety — coaches need to see you handling many kinds of situations, not just spectacular saves.

**Show:**
- **Shot-stopping variety.** Low corner saves, top corner saves, point-blank reactions, deflected shots. Not three of the same type.
- **1v1 saves.** The moment you come off your line against a forward bearing down.
- **Distribution.** Both feet for short play out, plus at least one accurate long ball. Modern coaches want a keeper who can be a build-up option.
- **Command of the box on crosses.** A clip where you come for a cross and claim it cleanly.
- **Footwork and positioning** — a save that's good because you were in the right spot, not because you made a desperation dive.
- **Reactions to deflections.** (Keepers who save the first shot and let the rebound in are a problem.)
- **Communication and leadership** — moments where you're visibly organizing the back line, calling for the ball on crosses, coaching defenders pre-set-piece.

**Skip:**
- Five-minute reels of only highlight saves with no distribution.
- Saves on shots that were going wide anyway.

## A note on who edits the video

If you can edit it yourself or have a parent who can, do it. If not, the market splits roughly three ways:

- **Basic editing** (you provide raw footage, they assemble a 3–5 minute reel) runs about **$150–$300**.
- **Premium edits** (with sections, title cards, multiple revisions) run **$400–$1,100**.
- **Full-service packages** that include videography and coach outreach run **$1,000–$5,000+**.

Whichever tier you pick, make sure *you* choose the clips. They don't know your kid. You do.

## Frequently asked questions

**How long should a college soccer highlight video be?**
Three to five minutes. Anything longer loses college coaches in the first 60 seconds. Lead with your best clips.

**Should I include music?**
Most college coaches mute videos by default, so music is at best neutral and at worst a distraction. Skip it.

**When should we make the first real highlight video?**
End of sophomore year of high school, or whenever your kid's playing style has stabilized into something a college coach could see two more years of development on top of. Videos made at 14 don't represent the player at 16.

**Goals only — or full game film?**
Neither. A highlight video shows your kid's best moments across multiple games — for forwards that includes goals, but also off-ball movement and first touches. For defenders, midfielders, and keepers, goals are bonus material, not the spine of the video.

**How much should a highlight video cost?**
A youth highlight video editing service typically charges $150–$300 for a basic 3–5 minute assembly from raw footage you provide. Premium edits with sections and revisions run $400–$1,100. Full-service packages with coach outreach run $1,000–$5,000+. You should pick the clips, not them — they don't know your kid.
