It's 6:55am on a Saturday and we're in the car heading to a tournament. My 13-year-old is in the back seat, AirPods in, jaw set like he's trying to look casual. He's not casual. I can tell by the way his knee is bouncing. He has a quarterfinal in 90 minutes against a club he's lost to twice this season, and he is *quietly losing his mind*.

If you've been a soccer parent for more than a season, you know this moment. You know the version of your kid that shows up in the car before a big game. And you know that what you say in the next 30 minutes — and especially in the 60 seconds before he walks onto the field — can either set him free or wind him tighter.

I'll be honest: I have said the wrong thing about a thousand times. I have said "just have fun!" with the absolute wrong energy. I've said "you've got this!" in a voice that meant *please please please you've got this because we drove four hours and I cannot watch this go poorly.* I've definitely said "play smart" before, as if my middle-schooler was going to suddenly remember what playing smart means at 7:55am with butterflies the size of pigeons.

So this is what I've learned. Some from sports psychologists, some from older parents in our club, and a lot from watching the moments backfire and trying again next weekend.

## Your kid is not who you think they are in this moment

The single biggest thing I got wrong early on was assuming every kid (and every pre-game) needs the same energy. They don't. There are roughly two kinds of pre-game kids, and the trick is knowing which one is sitting next to you.

**Hype-it-up kids.** These are the ones whose nerves *show*. They're quiet, they're a little pale, they aren't joking around like they usually do. They need their energy raised. Music. A high five. Reminders that their work has earned this moment. The pep talk that pumps them up.

**Calm-it-down kids.** These are the ones who already have too much energy. They're running their mouth, talking trash about the other team, kicking the back of your seat. They look amped. But underneath, they're spiraling. They need the volume turned *down*. Slower breathing, lower voice, "you've prepped for this — let your training take over."

The same kid can also be different kids on different days. My older one is mostly a Calm-it-down kid, but on tournament Sundays — when fatigue and pressure both peak — he flips Hype-it-up. The trick isn't knowing his "type." It's reading the version of him that morning.

## The 60 seconds before kickoff: say this, not that

You usually get one short window after warmups, before they walk on. Here's what works in our family and most of the families I've talked to.

| ✅ Say this | ❌ Don't say this |
|---|---|
| "Trust your training." | "Just have fun!" (the kid hears: *don't disappoint me*) |
| "Look for [your first touch / talking on D / one specific small thing]." | "Play smart." (vague, untranslatable mid-game) |
| "I love watching you play." | "Win this one." |
| "Whatever happens, I'm proud of how hard you worked to be here." | "Don't let them score." |
| (To the calm kid) "Take three breaths. You've got this." | (To the calm kid) "LET'S GOOOOOOO!" |
| (To the hype kid) "You've worked for this moment. Go get it." | (To the hype kid) "Don't be nervous." |
| Nothing. Sometimes silence + eye contact is the play. | Tactical instructions. That's the coach's job. |

The "I love watching you play" line specifically — I read this in a piece by a sports parenting speaker named Bruce Brown years ago, and I have repeated it as gospel ever since. It does something the other lines don't: it tells your kid the outcome doesn't change how you see them.

## What you say *after* the game matters even more

Here's the one I really got wrong for a long time. The post-game car ride is when most of the damage gets done, not the pre-game one.

The script that works: **"I love watching you play."** Repeat. Don't critique. Don't replay the moment they missed. Don't ask "what happened on that one play?" Don't, even gently, suggest they could have hustled more. The coach already has it covered. Your kid already knows what happened. The car ride home is not the time to coach.

If they want to talk about the game, let them lead. If they don't, put on music and let them decompress. Bring it up later in the week, when the loss is no longer raw.

## The deeper thing

The hardest part about being a soccer parent is that we want it for them so badly that the wanting starts to leak. They feel it. They internalize it. They start playing *for us* instead of for themselves, and when they're playing for us, the joy goes out of it — and then so does their best soccer.

The pre-game pep talk is the smallest possible version of this. Sixty seconds. One sentence. But it's where the leaking happens. If you can keep your wanting out of those sixty seconds, you've already done the parent thing right.

## Frequently asked questions

**My kid says they don't want to talk before games. What do I do?**
Honor it. The most important pre-game thing you can do is not force a pep talk your kid doesn't want. Silence and a hand on the shoulder is a complete sentence.

**What if they're really anxious and I don't know how to help?**
Don't dismiss the anxiety with "you'll be fine." Name what they're feeling ("makes sense that you're nervous — this is a big game") and then redirect to something small and specific they CAN control ("your first three touches — make them sharp").

**Is there a difference between game-day pep talks and tournament-weekend ones?**
Tournament weekends are about *managing fatigue and emotional load over multiple games*, not peaking once. Less hype, more recovery — nap, hydrate, low-stim time between games. The pep talk between games of a tournament is way shorter than the one before game 1.

**What about really young kids?**
Under-10s mostly just need to know you love them and that you're going to be on the sideline. They don't need tactical messages. They don't need motivational quotes. They need a juice box and "I'll be watching." That's it.
